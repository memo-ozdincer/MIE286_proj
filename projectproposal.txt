\section*{1.\enspace Background and Motivation}
\enspace Feedback is one of the most important interaction processes for a user in any UI design, from the quercus page shooting confetti when you submit something, to the hits in a video game. Feedback can be in different forms, like visual, tactile, audio... and Wickens' \textit{Multiple Resource Theory} posits these feedbacks use separate attentional channels and that when a visual task is paired with visual feedback interference increases [1,2].  
Cognitive Load Theory predicts that overloading one channel degrades performance, while distributing across modalities reduces load [3]. For rapid pointing tasks described by Fitts' Law [4,\,5], such interference may shift the \emph{speed--accuracy tradeoff} (SAT), which represents a relationship where faster responses incur more errors [6]. Prior work shows auditory feedback can reduce time in visual pointing tasks [7] and support motor learning without overloading the visual channel [8], but whether it changes the SAT \emph{structure}, and how participants might balance speed against accuracy, remains to be explored.

\section*{2.\enspace Research Question and Hypotheses}

\textbf{RQ:} Does the type of feedback (visual vs auditory) change our "SAT" in our visual-target selection task and does mixing task and feedback channel help with deloading feedback?
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{H\textsubscript{1} (Speed):} The average reaction time will be lower than other experiments when exposed to auditory feedback, because the brain prioritizes visual cues [1].
  \item \textbf{H\textsubscript{2} (Accuracy):} The auditory feedback will provide a larger average "radial" error, meaning that the increase in speed will cost us accuracy [6].
  \item \textbf{H\textsubscript{3} (Tradeoff):} The reaction time-error relationship will change. There will be a different tradeoff under auditory feedback [6].
\end{itemize}

\section*{3.\enspace Experimental Design and Methods}

\noindent\fcolorbox{accent!50}{accent!3}{%
\begin{minipage}{\dimexpr\linewidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}
\small\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.0}%
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}p{0.48\linewidth}X@{}}
\textbf{IV:} Feedback Type (Visual vs.\ Auditory) & \textbf{Design:} Within-subject; counterbalanced order\\
\textbf{DV\textsubscript{1}:} Response time (ms), onset to click & \textbf{Sample:} $N\!\ge\!24$ adults (18+); $\ge$12 per order\\
\textbf{DV\textsubscript{2}:} Radial error (px), click-to-center dist. & \textbf{Trials:} 2 blocks\,$\times$\,60 (+10 practice)\\
\end{tabularx}
\end{minipage}}
\\\\
\noindent\textbf{Participants.} $\ge$24 adults (18+) with normal/corrected vision and no hearing impairments, recruited via convenience sampling (classmates, friends, family). We record age range, device type (mouse/trackpad), and gaming frequency as covariates. Convenience-sampling limitations will be discussed in the report.

\noindent\textbf{Task.} We will set up target selection on the user's browser of canvas ($800\!\times\!500$\,px). They will see a circular target of radius 40\,px randomly on the screen; they will try to click on the target. The next target appears in 500\,ms; we log the coordinates and timestamps.

\noindent\textbf{Feedback conditions.}
\textcolor{olive}{\emph{Visual:} Flashing border (green for hit\,/\,red for miss, 120\,ms) + metrics on bottom (average RT, hit rate, mean radial error).} \textcolor{purple}{\emph{Auditory:} No Metrics (trial counter only); 200\,ms sound after click: high pitch\,=\,fast hit, medium\,=\,slow hit, low thud\,=\,miss.}

\noindent\textbf{Procedure ($\sim$5\,min).}
Consent\,$\to$\,instructions\,$\to$\,10 practice trials\,$\to$\,Block\,1 (60 trials)\,$\to$\, \\break\,$\to$\,Block\,2 (60 trials). Order either V$\to$A or\ A$\to$V.

\noindent\textbf{Controls.} Size of: target, canvas. $\#$ of trials, inter-trial interval, and instructions held constant. Randomize positions of targets. Accommodations per participant.

\section*{4.\enspace Analysis Plan (R/RStudio)}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Cleaning:} Exclude reaction time outliers (\,$<$\,200\,ms or $>$\,5\,s); verify num. trials.
  \item \textbf{Aggregation:} Collect average reaction time, mean radial error, hit rate for each.
  \item \textbf{Descriptives:} Means, SDs, medians; boxplots of RT and error; RT-vs-error scatterplot by condition.
  \item \textbf{Assumptions:} Shapiro--Wilk on within-subject differences for both DVs.
  \item \textbf{H\textsubscript{1}--H\textsubscript{2}:} Paired $t$-tests (Wilcoxon if non-normal); report $t$, $p$, Cohen's $d$.
  \item \textbf{H\textsubscript{3}:} Pearson $r$ (RT vs.\ error) per condition; \texttt{error\,$\sim$\,RT\,$\times$\,condition}, any significant interaction\,$=$\, will show different SAT slopes.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Limitations.} Convenience sampling limits generalizability; device variability and audio environments may add noise (recorded as covariates); fixed difficulty may produce accuracy ceiling effects.
